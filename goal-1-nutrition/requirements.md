## Key Nutrients to Prioritize in the Garden Plan

These are the hardest to source from a plant-based garden and deserve special attention:

1. **Complete protein** — most plants lack one or more essential amino acids. Combine legumes
   with grains or nuts. Eggs, dairy, or fish fill gaps easily.
2. **Omega-3 (EPA/DHA)** — plant sources only provide ALA. Consider keeping chickens, fish, or
   rabbits for complete coverage.
3. **Vitamin B12** — not available from plants. Requires animal products or supplementation.
4. **Vitamin D** — minimal food sources; sunlight is the primary source. Dried mushrooms exposed
   to UV light provide a plant-based option.
5. **Iodine** — absent from most garden soil unless amended. Sea kelp flakes or iodized salt needed.
6. **Selenium** — soil-dependent. 1–2 Brazil nuts per person per day covers the requirement entirely.
7. **Iron** — plant sources (non-heme) are less absorbed. Always pair iron-rich foods with vitamin C.
8. **Calcium** — achievable from greens (kale, rhubarb, bok choy) but large daily quantities required.

## Notes

- All values are based on average adults. Requirements shift with age, activity level, pregnancy,
  and health conditions.
- Water totals include water from food sources (fruits, vegetables, soups), not just drinking water.
- Omega-3 can be sourced from plants (ALA form) — flax, walnuts, perilla — but animal sources
  (eggs, fish) provide the more bioavailable EPA/DHA forms.
- Iron from plant sources (non-heme iron) is less bioavailable than from meat. Pair with vitamin C
  foods to improve absorption.
- Calcium from food is preferred over supplements for long-term health.
- Folate needs spike significantly during pregnancy — plan accordingly.
- Values sourced from USDA Dietary Reference Intakes (DRIs).
