Herbs

Herbs are among the most productive plants in a self-sufficient garden — they serve triple duty as food, medicine, and companions to other crops. This page covers every herb in the guide: what it does, how to use it, and how to prepare it. It is designed to grow as the guide grows.

Herb Reference

Herbs in the guide and their practical uses — culinary, medicinal, and companion roles.

Medicinal use note: The preparations listed here are traditional folk uses with a long safety record at normal culinary quantities. They are food-grade preparations, not substitutes for medical advice. Pregnant or nursing women and those on medication should check interactions before using medicinal doses of any herb.
Herb Uses Key Actions Simple Preparation Notes
Chamomile
MedicinalCompanion
 Calming, anti-inflammatory, digestive, sleep aid 1 tbsp fresh or 1 tsp dried flowers steeped 10 min in boiled water Self-seeds freely. Companion to brassicas and onions — improves their growth. Harvest flowers just after opening.
Thyme
CulinaryMedicinalCompanion
 Antibacterial, antifungal, expectorant, immune support Strong infusion (2 tsp dried/cup) with honey for coughs; use fresh in cooking Repels cabbage worm. Perennial once established. Thymol is one of the most studied antimicrobial plant compounds.
Sage
CulinaryMedicinalCompanion
 Antimicrobial, astringent, throat soothing, digestive Strong infusion gargled for sore throat; fresh in bean and squash dishes Repels cabbage moth, carrot fly. Perennial. Avoid medicinal doses during pregnancy.
Rosemary
CulinaryMedicinalCompanion
 Circulatory stimulant, memory, antimicrobial, antioxidant Infuse in oil or honey; use fresh in roasting and breads Repels carrot fly. Long-lived perennial shrub. Infused oil excellent for scalp and as a cooking oil.
Mint
CulinaryMedicinalCompanion
 Digestive, carminative, cooling, antibacterial Fresh or dried infusion after meals; add to water, sauces, and preserves Repels aphids and cabbage moth. Grow in a sunken pot — it spreads aggressively. Harvest before flowering for best flavour.
Lavender
CulinaryMedicinalCompanion
 Calming, sleep aid, antibacterial, pain relief (topical) Dried flowers in sachets; light infusion; small amounts in baking and honey Repels moths, deer, aphids. Dried bundles in linen repel clothes moths without chemicals. Bees love it throughout summer.
Calendula
MedicinalCompanion
 Anti-inflammatory, wound healing, skin repair (topical) Infused oil (petals in olive oil, 4–6 weeks); used in salves and skin treatments; edible petals in salads Long bloom — spring through hard frost. Attracts hoverflies and lacewings. One of the most useful medicinal plants for a homestead.
Comfrey
MedicinalCompanion
 Wound healing, bone and tissue repair (topical only), mineral accumulator Infused oil for topical use only; chop leaves as mulch or liquid fertilizer Do not take internally — contains pyrrolizidine alkaloids. Topical use is traditional and widely regarded as safe. Deep taproot mines calcium, potassium, phosphorus. Invaluable in the food forest.
Yarrow
MedicinalCompanion
 Wound-staunching, fever management, anti-inflammatory Fresh leaves applied to minor cuts to slow bleeding; strong infusion drunk warm to break a fever Self-seeds at bed edges. Attracts predatory wasps that eat caterpillars. Mineral accumulator. One of humanity's oldest medicinal herbs — found in Neanderthal burial sites.
Dill
CulinaryMedicinalCompanion
 Digestive, carminative (gas relief), antibacterial (seeds especially) Seeds chewed after meals; fronds used fresh; seed infusion for colic and indigestion Attracts parasitic wasps that control caterpillars. Let some plants flower and set seed — self-seeds freely. Do not plant near fennel (cross-pollinates).
Fennel
CulinaryMedicinal
 Digestive, carminative, milk production (traditional), expectorant Seeds in tea or chewed; fronds and bulb eaten fresh; seeds in bread and sausage Grow in isolation — allelopathic to most vegetables and cross-pollinates with dill. Every part is edible: seed, frond, bulb, flower, pollen. Attracts predatory insects.
Parsley
CulinaryMedicinal
 Nutrient-dense, diuretic, breath freshener, digestive Eaten fresh; infused as a mineral-rich tea; heavy in tabbouleh and chimichurri One of the most nutrient-dense herbs — high in vitamin K, vitamin C, and iron. Biennial — let it flower in year 2 to attract beneficial insects before going to seed.
Basil
CulinaryMedicinalCompanion
 Anti-inflammatory, antibacterial, adaptogen (tulsi varieties) Fresh in cooking; pesto; infused in oil or vinegar; tulsi basil as tea Repels aphids and whitefly near tomatoes. Annual — very frost-sensitive. Pinch flowers to extend harvest. Holy basil (Tulsi) has a different medicinal profile — adaptogenic and antiviral.
Chives
CulinaryCompanion
 Mild allium flavour, antifungal (in soil), circulatory Snip fresh over eggs, soups, potatoes; infuse flowers in vinegar for a purple herb vinegar Perennial. Repel aphids, carrot fly, apple scab. Flowers are edible and beautiful. One of the most useful "plant it once" herbs in the guide.
Borage
CulinaryCompanion
 Adrenal tonic (traditional), anti-inflammatory, bee magnet Edible blue flowers in salads, frozen in ice cubes, or as a garnish; young leaves in salads (cucumber flavour) One of the best bee plants in the garden — flowers all summer. Self-seeds prolifically once established. Bees work the flowers even in light rain.

Herb Teas & Simple Preparations

Basic preparations you can make from a working herb garden — teas, infused honeys, and a few classic remedies.

🌼 Traditional European · Ancient

Chamomile Evening Tisane

Chamomile · Mint · Honey

Ingredients

  • 1 tbsp fresh chamomile flowers (or 1 tsp dried)
  • A few fresh mint leaves (optional)
  • 250ml just-boiled water (not rolling boil)
  • 1 tsp honey to serve

Method

  1. Place flowers and mint in a mug or small teapot.
  2. Pour water at ~90°C (just off the boil) — boiling water destroys volatile oils.
  3. Cover and steep 5–10 min. Longer = stronger and slightly more bitter.
  4. Strain. Add honey after steeping to preserve its enzymes.
  5. Drink 30–45 min before bed. Can also be used as a warm compress for tired eyes.
Harvest chamomile flowers in the morning when just opened — this is when the essential oil content is highest. Dry surplus on a tray at room temperature for winter use.
🌿 Traditional European · Medieval

Thyme & Honey Oxymel

Thyme · Honey · Apple Cider Vinegar

Ingredients

  • 1 cup fresh thyme sprigs (or ½ cup dried)
  • 1 cup raw honey
  • 1 cup raw apple cider vinegar
  • Clean glass jar with non-metal lid

Method

  1. Pack thyme into a jar. Pour honey and vinegar over it; stir to combine.
  2. Seal. Shake daily for 4–6 weeks in a cool, dark place.
  3. Strain out herbs. Bottle the amber liquid.
  4. Take 1 tbsp straight or in warm water for coughs, sore throats, or as a daily tonic.
  5. Keeps 1 year at room temperature.
An oxymel (from the Greek for vinegar-honey) is one of the oldest medicinal preparations in Western herbalism, recorded by Hippocrates. The vinegar extracts different compounds than alcohol or water alone — making the preparation more complete. Replace thyme with rosemary for a warming, circulatory version.
🌱 Traditional worldwide · Ancient

Mint & Fennel Digestive Tea

Mint · Fennel Seed · Dill Seed

Ingredients

  • 1 tsp dried fennel seeds
  • ½ tsp dried dill seeds
  • A few fresh mint leaves
  • 250ml just-boiled water

Method

  1. Lightly crush fennel and dill seeds in a mortar — just crack them, don't grind.
  2. Add seeds and mint to a mug.
  3. Pour over just-boiled water. Cover and steep 8–10 min.
  4. Strain and drink after a heavy meal or when experiencing gas, bloating, or indigestion.
All three plants are carminatives — they relax smooth muscle in the gut and help expel gas. A version of this tea has been given to colicky infants as "gripe water" for centuries. Collect dill and fennel seeds in late summer by shaking flower heads into a paper bag.
🔥 North America / Europe · 19th century

Garden Fire Cider

Garlic · Onions · Rosemary · Thyme · Apple Cider Vinegar · Honey

Ingredients

  • ½ cup garlic, roughly chopped
  • ½ cup onion, roughly chopped
  • 2 tbsp fresh rosemary, roughly torn
  • 2 tbsp fresh thyme
  • 1 tbsp black peppercorns
  • 2 cups raw apple cider vinegar
  • 2–4 tbsp honey to finish

Method

  1. Combine all solids in a clean glass jar. Press down.
  2. Pour vinegar over to fully cover. Seal with a non-metal lid (vinegar corrodes metal).
  3. Shake daily; steep 4 weeks in a cool, dark place.
  4. Strain well through muslin. Stir honey into the liquid to taste.
  5. Take 1 tbsp daily as an immune tonic. Dilute in warm water or take straight.
Traditional versions include horseradish and ginger root — add if you have them. Fire cider keeps 1 year at room temperature and 2+ years refrigerated. It's a concentrated extract of some of the most antimicrobial plants in the guide.
🌿 Traditional European · Medieval

Sage Throat Tea & Gargle

Sage · Honey · Apple Cider Vinegar

Ingredients

  • 6–8 fresh sage leaves (or 1 tbsp dried)
  • 250ml just-boiled water
  • 1 tsp honey
  • 1 tsp apple cider vinegar (for gargle)

Method

  1. Steep sage in just-boiled water, covered, for 10–15 min (longer than most teas — you want it strong).
  2. Add honey; stir until dissolved.
  3. As a tea: drink warm, slowly, sipping through the throat.
  4. As a gargle: cool to lukewarm, add vinegar, gargle for 30 seconds at a time, 3–4× daily.
Clinical studies have confirmed sage's efficacy for sore throat — comparable to some pharmaceutical sprays in small trials. The German Commission E (Europe's herb regulatory body) officially endorses sage for throat and mouth inflammation. Harvest leaves before the plant flowers for the highest essential oil content.
🌸 Traditional European · Ancient

Calendula Infused Oil

Calendula · Olive Oil

Ingredients

  • 1 cup dried calendula petals (fully dried — no moisture)
  • 2 cups cold-pressed olive oil
  • Clean glass jar

Method

  1. Solar method (preferred): Pack petals into jar; cover completely with oil. Seal; place in a sunny window 4–6 weeks, shaking daily.
  2. Quick method: Combine in a small saucepan; heat on lowest setting for 4–6 hours. Do not simmer — you want infusion, not frying.
  3. Strain through muslin; squeeze out all oil. Bottle in dark glass.
  4. Use directly on dry skin, minor burns, cuts, rashes, and chapped lips.
  5. To make a salve: melt 1 oz beeswax per cup of infused oil; pour into tins; cool.
Petals must be fully dried before infusing — any moisture will cause the oil to mould. Harvest flowers on a dry afternoon; dry 1–2 weeks on a tray before use. Calendula's anti-inflammatory action comes from triterpenoids and flavonoids; these are oil-soluble, which is why infused oil works so well. Keeps 1 year in a cool dark place.
🍯 Traditional European · Ancient

Herbed Honey

Honey · Thyme · Rosemary · Lavender

Ingredients (per jar)

  • 1 cup raw honey
  • Choose one or combine:
    • 2 tbsp fresh thyme sprigs
    • 1 tbsp fresh rosemary
    • 1 tbsp dried lavender flowers

Method

  1. Press herbs into a clean jar. Pour honey over; stir to coat.
  2. Leave at room temperature 1–2 weeks — no heat needed, honey is self-preserving.
  3. Use infused or strain out herbs for a cleaner product.
  4. Thyme honey: drizzle on cheese, in tea, or take by the spoon for coughs.
  5. Lavender honey: on bread, in hot milk, or as a sleep aid before bed.
Honey preserves herbs indefinitely — ancient honey found in Egyptian tombs was still antimicrobially active. No refrigeration needed; honey's low water activity prevents microbial growth. Raw honey is important — pasteurized honey lacks the enzymes and some antimicrobial compounds.
